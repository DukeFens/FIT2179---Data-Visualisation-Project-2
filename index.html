Part 1 · The Science

Why Your Body Loves Badminton

Badminton is far more than a backyard pastime. Research shows it is one of the most effective full-body workouts available — and one of the very few sports scientifically linked to a longer life.

Muscles Fired in a Single Smash

Each dot represents a muscle group activated during a forehand overhead smash. Bigger and redder = working harder. The wrist and chest drive the shuttle — but your legs and core are just as critical.

Source: Frontiers in Sports and Active Living (2025) · PMC12170632

Play Badminton, Live Longer

The Copenhagen City Heart Study tracked 8,577 people for 25 years. Badminton players outlived sedentary peers by 6.2 years — nearly double the benefit of running.

Source: Schnohr et al. (2018) · Mayo Clinic Proceedings

Badminton vs. Your Day — Calorie Comparison

A competitive game burns roughly the same calories as running at 8 km/h — but feels far less like work. Even a social hit burns more than three hours of office coding.

Source: Compendium of Physical Activities (Ainsworth et al., 2011)

Part 2 · The Nation

Half a Million Australians Already Know

AusPlay 2025 data shows over 420,000 Australians participate in badminton regularly. Victoria leads the nation — and university suburbs cluster with courts.

Where Does Australia Play? (National Map)

Hover over each state to see participation rates, total players, and registered clubs. Victoria and New South Wales hold the largest communities, driven by multicultural urban populations.

Source: AusPlay 2025, Australian Sports Commission (released 30 April 2026)

📈 Did you know? Victoria's participation rate of 2.4% is the highest in Australia — partly explained by Melbourne's large Southeast Asian community, for whom badminton is a deeply embedded cultural sport.

A Sport on the Rise

Participation has grown steadily over the decade, recovering strongly after the COVID-19 dip. Note: AusPlay changed its survey methodology in 2023 (dashed line), so earlier and later figures use different collection methods.

Source: AusPlay 2016–2025 & Badminton Australia Annual Reports

Gen Z Is Taking Over the Courts

The 18–24 age bracket — squarely university age — is by far the largest group of badminton players in Australia. Click the legend to filter by gender.

Source: AusPlay 2025 (By Sport Data Tables), Australian Sports Commission

Find Your Court in Melbourne

Sixteen badminton venues are mapped across Greater Melbourne. Coloured dots indicate venues near major universities — courts are genuinely accessible for students at Monash, RMIT, and the University of Melbourne.

Source: Badminton Victoria club directory & OpenStreetMap contributors

Part 3 · The Elite

From School Yards to the World Stage

Australia's competitive ecosystem runs from 40,000 Sporting Schools participants all the way up to twenty Senior Falcons representing Australia on the global circuit.

The Talent Pipeline

The funnel from grassroots participation to the Senior Falcons is steep. Of the 40,000+ children who try badminton through Shuttle Smash, fewer than 20 will ever wear the national uniform. Hover each bar for program details.

Source: Badminton Australia Annual Report 2023/24 · BWF international results

Climbing the World Rankings

Australia's top players have made steady gains on the BWF circuit. Setyana Mapasa and Angela Yu's rise to a top-10 Women's Doubles ranking culminated in the Paris 2024 Olympics. Click a player name to highlight their line.

Source: BWF Historical Rankings (corporate.bwfbadminton.com)

Australia's Tournament Ecosystem

The Australian Badminton Open has grown from a Super 300 to a Super 500 event, attracting elite global players to Sydney each year. Bubble size reflects total tournament entries.

Source: BWF World Tour official results · Badminton Australia

🏸 The takeaway: Whether you're at Monash, RMIT, or the University of Melbourne — a badminton court is minutes away. The science says it will add years to your life. The data says hundreds of thousands of Australians have already figured that out.
